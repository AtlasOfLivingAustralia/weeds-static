Each Australian state and territory has legislation protecting flora and fauna. There is also national legislation. The classification systems used to indicate the degree of threat that species are under varies from jurisdiction to jurisdiction. In addition, Australian species are also listed on The International Union for Conservation of Nature Red List. The following summarises the major classification systems in use and provides links to additional information where possible.

International

The International Union for Conservation of Nature produces the  IUCN Red List of Threatened Species™. The Red List uses the following classification codes:

  • Extinct
  • Extinct in the wild
  • Critically endangered
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Near threatened
  • Least concern
  • Data deficient
  • Not evaluated

Learn more about the classifications by the IUCN.

Australia wide

Classification codes under the Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act 1999:

  • Extinct
  • Extinct in the wild
  • Critically Endangered
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Conservation dependent

Learn more about the classifications used by the Department of Sustainability, Environment, Water, Population and Communities.

Australian Capital Territory

Classification codes under The Nature Conservation Act 1980:

  • endangered
  • vulnerable

Learn more about the classifications used by the Department of Territory and Municipal Services.

New South Wales

Classification codes under the Threatened Species Conservation Act 1995:

  • Presumed Extinct
  • Critically Endangered
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Endangered Population

Learn more about the classifications used by the Department of Environment, Climate Change and Water

Northern Territory

Classification codes under the Territory Parks and Wildlife Conservation Act:

  • Extinct in the Wild
  • Critically Endangered
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Near threatened
  • Data deficient

Learn more about the classifications used by the Department of Natural Resources, Environment, the Arts and Sports.

Queensland

Classification codes under the Nature Conservation Act 1992:

  • Extinct in the wild
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Near threatened
  • Least concern

Learn more about the classifications used by the Department of Environment and Resource Management.

South Australia

Classification codes under the National Parks and Wildlife Act 1972:

  • Extinct
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Rare

Learn more about the classifications used by the Department of Environment and Natural Resources.

Tasmania

Classification codes under the Threatened Species Protection Act 1995:

  • Extinct
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Rare

Learn more about the classifications used by the Department of Primary Industries, Parks, Water and Environment .

Victoria

The Flora and Fauna Guarantee Act 1988 (FFG) lists threatened species but does not provide sub-classifications. The FFG Threatened List is based on public nominations and is therefore not comprehensive. The Department of Sustainability and Environment maintains comprehensive, non-statutory Advisory Lists of rare or threatened Victorian plants and fauna. The Atlas shows the Advisory List classification for species considered of conservation significance in Victoria.

Classification codes defined in the Advisory List of Rare or Threatened Plants in Victoria:

  • Presumed Extinct
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Rare
  • Poorly known

Classification codes defined in the Advisory List of Threatened Vertebrate Fauna in Victoria:

  • Extinct
  • Regionally Extinct
  • Extinct in the Wild
  • Critically Endangered
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Near Threatened
  • Data Deficient

Classification codes defined in the Advisory List of Threatened Invertebrate Fauna in Victoria:

  • Extinct
  • Regionally Extinct
  • Extinct in the Wild
  • Critically Endangered
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Near Threatened
  • Data Deficient

Western Australia

The Wildlife Conservation Act 1950 lists threatened species and only differentiates between Presumed Extinct and Threatened.
The species lists can be found here Listing of species and ecological communities.
The legal terminology for threatened flora is Declared Rare Flora. Under State policy, IUCN criteria are used to differentiate the threat ranking of listed threatened species. Additionally, under State policy, poorly known or data deficient species, rare but not threatened species, and conservation dependent species are listed as Priority Species. The Atlas shows species threat ranking and Priority status.

Classification codes under State policy:

  • Presumed Extinct
  • Critically Endangered
  • Endangered
  • Vulnerable
  • Priority One: Poorly known taxa with few populations on threatened lands.
  • Priority Two: Poorly known taxa with few populations including some on conservation lands.
  • Priority Three: Poorly known taxa with several or widespread populations.
  • Priority Four: Rare but not threatened taxa in need of monitoring.
  • Priority Five: Conservation dependent taxa that are dependent on ongoing active management.

Learn more about the classifications used by the Department of Environment and Conservation.
