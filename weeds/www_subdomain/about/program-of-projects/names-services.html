Australian National Species Lists - Accessible authoritative species names

Background

Management of biodiversity data depends on the existence of an authoritative taxonomic framework to which any data item including a scientific name can be related. The ideal taxonomic framework would contain all published scientific names, all common names, an up-to-date classification based on the best available taxonomy, indicate whether a name is accepted as the current name for a species or considered to be some kind of synonym, and a set of mappings between this classification and other checklists of importance to the community (e.g. classifications used in state floras, red lists, CITES lists, etc.).

There is currently no single resource that includes all of this information. Indeed relevant information is scattered through 250 years of printed literature. In more recent times, some of this information has been consolidated for some taxonomic groups in online resources, but there are still many gaps and much inconsistency in the information.

The Atlas of Living Australia is addressing the identified need by commissioning the Australian National Species Lists (NSL) project which aims to fill major gaps in nomenclatural and taxonomic data and improve the infrastructure for storing, managing and delivering this data to users. This includes providing access to accurate currently accepted names and synonymies.

Project Overview

The Atlas is partnering with the Integrated Biodiversity Information Systems Team (IBIS) at the Australian National Botanic Gardens (ANBG), the Australian Biological Resources Study (ABRS), and the Council of Heads of Australasian Herbaria (CHAH) on the NSL Project to produce the most comprehensive, current and authoritative checklist of Australian species ever produced. The ANBG is also collaborating with major international authorities responsible for maintaining international species checklists, and is a contributor to major international projects which are aiming to build a checklist of all organisms in the world.

This checklist of Australian species will be accessible to the scientific & research communities, policy makers, natural resource managers, and the general public and will allow everyone working with species names to have confidence that it is the most authoritative and up-to-date information available.

The Atlas will be able to manage aggregation of the vast majority of available biodiversity data by making use of the Australian Plant Census (APC) and the Australian Faunal Directory (AFD) as the most complete and current taxonomic checklists for Australian plant and animal species.

The NSL Project involves two major components:

  1. The ANBG will develop the information management infrastructure underpinning the taxonomic work and the delivery of checklist data to consumers through a set of web services. This work involves several components, each of which will be developed and implemented incrementally over the remaining two years of ALA funding, gradually increasing in functionality and improving in usability. The aim of this approach is to deliver early capability and improve it over time in line with evolving priorities and available resourcing.
  2. ABRS and CHAH are co-ordinating taxonomic work with a focus on filling out as much as possible, known gaps in the description, naming and classification of Australian organisms. This work is expected to add approximately 41,000 new species to the checklist over the remaining two years of ALA funding, increasing the number of known named species to about 188,000, 33.2% of the estimated 567,000 total number of species in Australia. This work will also include updates to about 77,000 existing names.It is recognised that taxonomic work is ongoing and that resourcing in this area will still be required beyond the Atlas project. However the Atlas is making a significant contribution to accelerating this work.

The components of the ANBG work include:

  • Development of a major new database for storing all names in the context of where they occur and how they are used, and their relationships to other names. This database will consolidate into a standardised format and structure, the myriad separate databases and documents (see below) which are currently used for managing species names data;
  • Development of new interfaces and tools for taxonomists to be able to work with the authoritative names in the new data store; and
  • Development of a set of web services to deliver names, classification and other species information to people who need to use the data.

The components of the ABRS and CHAH work include:

ABRS is managing taxonomic work for the fauna, marine algae and lichens projects, whilst CHAH is managing the vascular plants, fungi and other non-vascular groups. Freshwater algae will be updated through an ABRS/CHAH collaboration with the Botanic Gardens Trust, Sydney, where the current Census of Freshwater Algae of Australia database is housed. Integration of the marine algae and freshwater algae data will be managed by ABRS.

CHAH will also complete the Australian Plant Census for vascular plants a project known as the consensus census. This project has its foundations in the Australia’s Virtual Herbarium project (AVH) and will run as a collaborative project between all State Herbaria led by the Centre for Plant Biodiversity Research (CPBR).

Fungi lists will be developed from the Interactive Catalogue of Australian Fungi database housed at the Royal Botanic Gardens Melbourne. This project will be led by Tom May in conjunction with an Australia-wide expert group.

The mosses will be developed from AusMoss, a catalogue of Australian mosses and the liverworts and hornworts will be developed from the ABRS checklist.
