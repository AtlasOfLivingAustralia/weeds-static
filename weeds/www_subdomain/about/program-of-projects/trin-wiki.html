TRIN Overview

The CERF Taxonomy Research & Information Network (TRIN) is a group of Australian taxonomists working together to address gaps in our knowledge of Australia’s biodiversity. Taxonomy is the discovery, description, identification & classification of organisms. Taxonomy provides the basis for identifying and monitoring Australia’s biodiversity which in turn provides the knowledge needed for effective environmental management.

TRIN aims to enhance and accelerate taxonomic research and communication on Australia’s biodiversity, with broad goals to:

  • reinvigorate taxonomy in Australia
  • evaluate and road test new methodologies for research and delivery of taxonomic information for a wide range of end users
  • create and maintain a modern collaborative national electronic framework for taxonomic knowledge exchange.

A TRIN Project, What Bug Is That?

What is the TRIN Wiki?

The TRIN Wiki is a collaborative platform for members of TRIN and the broader taxonomic community. Anyone is welcome to participate; use TRIN’s on-line collaboration and communcation tools (website, wiki, email lists, etc.), or initiate new collaborative taxonomy research projects.

The TRIN Wiki is organised around specific projects which vary in their maturity and target audience. Some contain information put together for public consumption. Others are moderated or closed spaces, in which taxonomic projects collaborate on their own business prior to it being published.

Projects include Australian Ants, Mayflies and Aquatic Invertebrates, Insect Families on the Web, Small Terrestrial Vertebrates, Environmental Weeds, Biodiversity Informatics, Communication & Knowledge Exchange, and Australian Mangrove & Saltmarsh Species

New Contributors Welcome

TRIN is seeking to engage with more researchers and natural historians to refine our toolkits and wiki. In return, TRIN will provide users with the tools to compile content tailored to their needs.

Tools for Creating New Knowledge

The Problem

The building of biodiversity knowledge is necessarily slow. Expertise is limited and there are a large number of taxa which are yet to be described in more detail than those given in their original (formal taxon) description. Additional knowledge accumulates very slowly and may not be revised for decades. Biodiversity knowledge which is both truly ‘born digital’ and online is rare. And to be structured in a way that enables it to be mobilised and re-mixed in new contexts is rarer still.

The Taxon Profile Toolkit

This project provides collaborative online tools with simple interfaces for authors who wish to create, collate, or compile well-structured biodiversity knowledge.  The intention is to be flexible and non-prescriptive about how content is structured and at the same time introduce strong (semantic) rigour and transportability so that information can (as required and as permitted) be moved, and effectively reused in novel contexts with the meaning preserved.  It will be simple to port large quantities of knowledge into this toolkit from other sources and to get it out afterwards.  It will be simple to make links (as needed) to data provided from online biodiversity services and data sources.

This project aims to develop tools that are needed and will be used. Prototypes are ready and deployed. TRIN Wiki is seeking to engage with more researchers and natural historians so as to further refine the toolkit and wiki. TRIN Wiki will provide users with the tools to compile content in return for their assistance in making the tools better fit the needs of biodiversity research and publishing.

What are Taxon Profiles?

“A term for - the many and varied - structured templates for information associated with a group of organisms, also known in a narrower context as Species Fact Sheets and Species Pages.”

Species profile of Geopelia humeralisPhoto courtesy of Garry Jolley-Rogers, TRIN Wiki

Biodiversity information is typically highly structured. Information is separately presented for each Taxon of interest (e.g. species, genus, or family) in a consistent, template like form. Sections may be identified by labels, or typographically (in written works), or via layout. Almost any kind of information relevant to the organisms under consideration may be included in a profile. In some form, all profiles include a name; most also include some nomenclature and identifying attributes.

Labels, terminology and meaning are generally consistent for a given group or work (be that monograph or website). However, the organisation and labelling of other information can be idiosyncratic using conventions and language particular to a given biological discipline. This presents challenges - particularly if work is to be used in applications outside its original context; and thus a challenge for projects which present biological information from many sources into new forms (e.g. aggregators such as the Atlas of Living Australia, Encyclopaedia of Life)

A Challenge

There is no agreed form of a Taxon (or more narrowly, species) Profile, Fact Sheet or Page. There is unlikely to ever be one.

The terminology and organisation of biodiversity knowledge differs with each discipline. This is not just contingency. It is also well founded scientific necessity. The means of identification differs with different kinds of organisms; and different facts are of interest. Formats, content, and labels can vary considerably in publications which describe the same taxonomic groups. Just as authors interests vary so do the facts they have on hand. Frequently, different applications of terminology are also apparent; a consequence of history or differing scientific views and discipline specific vocabularies.

TRIN Wiki Solution

Wallace Core mappings for TRIN Wiki.Photo courtesy of Garry Jolley-Rogers.

TRIN Wiki is building a meta-taxon profile (Wallace Core); a single expansive framework capable of describing many arbitrary taxon profiles, and the means to use it to describe individual/given taxon profiles.

This is essential for the use of information in novel contexts; and as a means to standardise general biodiversity knowledge (as opposed to just for nomenclatural or identification information) without the necessity of changing content, labels or organisation.

Wallace Core will be based on a wide-ranging sample of past and current books, journals and websites; and as such will be a summary of best and current practice of how to organise biodiversity facts; in forms appropriate to discipline, taxon, and purpose.

Current Achievements

Prototypes are ready and deployed.

Development of ‘The Taxon Profile Toolkit’ continues in the TRIN Wiki including online rich text editors that produce versioned, structured content.

There are flexible user defined templates for biodiversity knowledge including citation management, pictures and media.  Work continues to make the creation and application of templates more user friendly.

The framework and underlying super-structure of ‘Wallace Core’ is complete. A survey is underway of taxon profiles (e.g. species pages and fact sheets) chosen to be representative of content across the biological disciplines.  Wallace Core will be built from this survey. Its constituents defined so as to be broad yet rigorously defined and foremost of all usable.

Deliverables & Timeline

The Taxon Profile Toolkit will provide novel techniques for the:

  • compilation
  • collation
  • collaboration
  • structuring
  • publishing
    • online
    • in print
    • to a database
  • mark up
  • exchange

of biodiversity knowledge  - from current research and existing sources.

It will include tools and templates for the compilation of taxonomic knowledge with online editors which is versioned, collaborative, structured in content. In a form that is:

  • well structured
  • flexible
  • tolerant of  idiosyncrasy
  • portable
  • re-usable (as appropriate)
  • linked to other Biodiversity Resources (nomenclators, keys, images).

There will be a means so that:

  • access can be appropriately controlled
  • content can be easily ported from other sources
  • content can be exported in a form that can be digested elsewhere
  • to translate and link information about taxa across disciplines
  • to encode similar/common meaning to elements

And:

  • an exchange standard for biodiversity knowledge.

The TRIN Wiki project is progressively refining The Taxon Profile Toolkit associated with TRIN WIKI and Wallace Core.
