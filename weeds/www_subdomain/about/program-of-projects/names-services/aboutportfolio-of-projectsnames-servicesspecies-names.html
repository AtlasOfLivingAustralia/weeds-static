What is a Species Name?

Species names are the labels which we use to refer to real life organisms. A name label is permanently and uniquely assigned to a representative specimen (“type” specimen) to name all members of a group of organisms which share the same set of morphological characteristics. Genetic characteristics are also playing an increasingly important role in species differentiation. Species names are given in latinised language and consist of at least 2 parts: a genus, and a species epithet (eg. Eucalyptus blakelyi). They may also have other parts such as sub-species epithet, form, variety, cultivar, strain, etc. Whenever a species name is used, it should also be accompanied by the author of the name and it’s taxonomic usage as this confirms the context for the use of the name - referred to as a “taxon concept”. This has particular significance in name taxonomy. The full scientific name for the above example is: Eucalyptus blakelyi Maiden var. blakelyi. It’s common or vernacular names are Blakely’s Red Gum or Hill Red Gum.

Scientific names are determined by taxonomists according to rules specified by international nomenclatural codes which vary across different taxonomic groups. The rules which govern these names are quite stable, but the grouping of organisms which are represented by a “type” specimen can change over time as we learn more and our understanding of the characteristics and assumptions used in classifying organisms changes. Such changes can result in groupings being split, merged, amalgamated, or moved to different places in the taxonomic tree. This may cause the names used to represent the different arrangement of organism groups to be changed. Previous names are referred to as synonyms of the current name. It is important to keep a history of changes to names so that one can navigate from a previously known name to a current name and vice-versa.

Different scientists can also use different characteristics, criteria and assumptions to classify organisms into homogeneous groups and/or the taxonomic tree, and this can result in different, but equally valid, uses of the same name (ie. different taxon concepts). In order to overcome some of the problems that this causes for interpretation of a name, groups of eminent scientists make decisions about which particular classifications for different groups of organisms should be “accepted” for general use of names in those groups. The Australian Plant Census (APC) and Australian Faunal Directory (AFD) represent the accepted classifications of species names for Australian plants and animals respectively.

Common names such as grey kangaroo are useful as a colloquial label that most of us can understand immediately, but they should not be used as a substitute for a scientific name when precision is needed such as when identifying an organism for an occurrence record, taxonomic work, or in matters of conservation or biosecurity, etc. because common names do not have the curatorial controls that scientific names do. For example, there are two species of grey kangaroo, the Eastern Grey Kangaroo ( Macropus giganteus Shaw, 1790) and the Western Grey Kangaroo ( Macropus fuliginosus (Desmarest, 1817)) - which one do we mean when we refer to “grey kangaroo”?. Also, one species may be referred to by several different common names. For example, the plant Echium plantagineum L. has an extensive range across southern Australia and is known in various places as either Patterson’s Curse, Salvation Jane, Southern Bluebell, Riverina Bluebell, blue weed, Purple Viper’s Bugloss, or purple bugloss. When one of these names is used that is not familiar to a reader/listener, they will not know what is being discussed or will make incorrect assumptions about it.
