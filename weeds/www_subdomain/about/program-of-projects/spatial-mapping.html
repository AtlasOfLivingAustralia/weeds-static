The Spatial Portal

Lee Belbin - Geospatial Team Leader
Lee Belbin has just been appointed as the Team Leader for Geospatial Data Management in the Atlas of Living Australia. Lee’s background has been in geosciences, quantitative ecology, conservation evaluation and project, science and data management.

“Much of the data that the Atlas will be delivering will have a spatial component; a link to a location where for example a species has been observed or an environment has been sampled in some way. An Atlas is inherently spatial. This places great emphasis on developing a ‘spatial portal‘ that is intuitive to use and effective in delivering and capturing a wide range of information about Australia’s living things.”

“There is also a need to bring together in this portal a range of ‘environmental’ data that will help to add value to the living data. These layers could be used for analyses or more simply (yet effective) as basemaps. For example, mean annual temperature could be used to help determine where a species could be found, or a layer such as a soil classification could be used as a basemap for displaying observations of a plant species. Having ready access to useful environmental data is a big task, but one that has great value for helping address the broadest range of environmental priorities.”

The Problem

There is no single public portal on the Web that integrates and maps all the following for the Australian region-

  1. All terrestrial, marine and limnetic species
  2. A comprehensive suite of environmental data layers that may affect the distribution of species (e.g., mean annual temperature)
  3. Contextual layers that can be used to analyse species distributions (e.g., land-use and land tenure)
  4. Over 500,000 geographic point and polygonal features
  5. Robust analysis of biological and environmental data
  6. The download of complete records of mapped biological observations and associated environmental and contextual data
  7. A comprehensive User Needs Analysis identified two priorities for a national spatial portal to Australia’s biological data
    1. Where do these species occur?
    2. What species occur in this area?

Why this is so important

Hundreds of millions of dollars have been spent on the collection of biological data by Federal and State governments, research institutions, industry, interest groups and citizens. These data are at various levels of discoverability, availability and in a wide range of digital and non-digital formats. These data are not integrated and most are not publicly accessible.

Providing a ‘one-stop-shop’ that gathers and integrates these data will be of immense value for knowledge discovery, conservation and land planning and management, education at all levels, biosecurity and for research.

Organizations such as Geoscience Australia, the Bureau of Meteorology, CSIRO, the Bureau of Rural Studies and many other agencies have collected a wide range of environmental data that affects the distribution of species. These data have not however been integrated and made publicly available in a form amendable to all potential needs. Such integration and wide availability is required if the potential value of Australia’s biological data is to be realized. Physical and chemical characteristics of our environment affect our natural ecosystems, the human environment, agriculture and fishing. It is therefore vital that a web site exists that integrates and maps all aspects of our environment and associated biological observations.

A proportion of any data will have errors that may cause significant problems in analysis if not addressed. Such errors are more likely to be recognised if they are integrated with related data and widely discoverable. Education, research, land planning and management, biosecurity, agriculture and fisheries will benefit from access to the broadest database.

The Solution

  1. Integrate all biological data that has a location and a species name of any type. This includes point observations, maps of species distributions base on expert opinion and maps of estimates of biodiversity.
  2. Gather Australian experts to identify priority environmental data layers that are thought to have some influence on the distribution of terrestrial, marine and limnetic species. Examples include rate of evaporation, soil characteristics, salinity and drainage.
  3. Identify data layers that could provide a useful context in the understanding of the biodiversity of the Australian region. Examples include land-use, land-tenure, bioregionalizations, catchment and local government areas.
  4. Provide simple and open access to over 500,000 geographic features of the Australian region. These include all point features from the Australian Gazetteer and additional polygonal features sourced from various international web sites. These features enable ready zoom to location within the spatial portal.
  5. Negotiate access to the data custodians to obtain, integrate and make the data openly and easily accessible.
  6. Add value to the data by providing a systematic and extensive infrastructure that extends beyond data integration. Examples include the provision of an effective classification of data layers, the use of globally unique identifiers for all aspects of the data in the Atlas.
  7. Design a spatial portal based on the best known models of Google Maps and Google Earth. The spatial portal retains the Google ‘look and feel’ and the use of the Google base maps.
  8. Provide a range of web services that will facilitate access to information on the spatial portal from the widest range of applications. Examples include a web map service and a web feature service to provide maps of, and data in the spatial portal.
  9. Provide exemplars of spatial analysis that uses biological and environmental data. Examples include spatial modelling (transforming point observations of species into mapped probabilities), and the generation of environmental classifications. Priority methods were recommended at a workshop of domain experts.
  10. Enable downloads of fundamental and derived data. The spatial portal will not be able to provide analytical tools that will address all requirements. It can however provide integrated biological, environmental, contextual and biodiversity data in a format that can be readily ingested into the widest range of applications. Communities can then use their favourite analytical techniques on the data provided by the Atlas.
  11. The integration of biological data and environmental data facilitates the identification of data errors. For example, mapping all observations of a species (or higher level taxonomy) will often identify location or taxonomic outliers. Errors can also be identified by examining the distribution of biological observations within their corresponding environmental envelopes. This type of analysis is not possible without tight integration of biological and environmental data.
  12. Enable the mapping of the distributions of biota at species, genus, family, class and order-level.
  13. Allow for the widest definition of area in addressing the user-need of “what species are in this area”. Options include
    1. Visible map area
    2. User-defined and digitized rectangle
    3. User-defined and digitized polygon
    4. User-defined and digitized point and radius
    5. User defined street address and radius
    6. Predefined unique polygon (e.g., a State or local government area)
    7. Predefined non-unique polygon (e.g., land-use = agriculture, land-tenure = park/reserve)
  14. Use and Free and Open Source (FOSS) philosophy in the design and building of the spatial portal. The Atlas Spatial Portal is based on the IMOS spatial portal. The Java code base allows for maximum flexibility of deployed systems and code development. Like the IMOS portal, the Atlas Spatial Portal code and development platform is in the public domain.
  15. Base the spatial portal on the Best Current Practice. The Spatial Portal uses the Google ‘look and feel’ and basemaps that most people are familiar with.
  16. Wherever possible use international standards in the development of Atlas services. Examples include WMS, WFS, WCS, LSIDs, ISO19115, ANZLIC and the Marine Community Profile, WGS84 and others.

Strategy

The strategy taken by the Spatial Portal is based on

  1. The User Need’s Analysis based on extensive surveys of the potential Atlas client community.  Subsequent discussions with an increasing catchment of potential clients has built on that analysis.
  2. Feedback from extensive user testing and demonstrations of the alpha and beta spatial portal.
  3. Extensive evaluation over 50 web map portals that deliver some form of biological and/or environmental data.
  4. Broad acknowledgement in the web mapping sector of the utility and efficiency of a free and open source development strategy based on international and national standards.
  5. Two workshops of domain experts to identify effective spatial analytical methods, and environmental and contextual data layers best suited the the Atlas environment.
  6. An extensive and intuitive web-based portal and associated web services address the widest range of applications requiring access to a comprehensive data of biological, environmental and contextual data.

The Atlas Geospatial Data Management Project

The primary objectives:

Objective 1      Provide an efficient and effective web-based portal to answer the question “where is these species found?” and “what species occur in this area?” 
Objective 2      Build a National Environmental Data Library that contains terrestrial, marine and limnetic data layers that can address the widest range of environmental issues by all Australian communities of interest.
Objective 3     Provide a flexible and efficient data download and web services to spatial data in the Atlas.
Objective 4     Build a suite of state-of-the-art analytical tools that demonstrate the utility of integrated biological, environmental, and contextual data.Objective 5     Collaborate wherever possible with related FOSS spatial portal and data projects.

Achievements

  • A spatial portal based on the Google Maps/Earth “look and feel” and basemaps
  • An operational spatial portal that addresses “Where does this species occur?” and “What species occurs in this area”?
  • Generalization of species in the above to genus, family, class and order and of the definition of area to 6 options
  • Ability to map x million biological observations
  • Access to 300 environmental and contextual layers in the National Environmental Data Library (NEDL). These layers can all be mapped and used in a range of analytical techniques.
  • A two-level classification of data layers in the NEDL for mapping and analysis.
  • Integration of metadata for data layers within the NEDL.
  • A comprehensive data download service for biological and associated environmental and contextual data.
  • The ability to map over 500,000 geographic features, both point and polygon
  • A robust spatial prediction tool to transform species point observations to probability surfaces
  • An effective classification tool that will accept any combination of ~250 environmental surfaces and create an environmental classification (environmental domains).
  • Web services for map images (WMS) and the gazetteer database of geographic features.
  • A flexible map printing function that allows of a range of output resolutions and formats
