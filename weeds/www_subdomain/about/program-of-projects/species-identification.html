IdentifyLife

Latest News: IdentifyLife launched at the International Botanic Congress on the 28th July 2011. Read about it »

IdentifyLife website: http://www.identifylife.org/

The Problem

An enormous amount of descriptive information about organisms, ranging from natural-language descriptions to identification keys to coded descriptive data, exists on the web and in the published and unpublished literature. However, the information is widely dispersed, usually poorly managed, and often difficult to find.
Theoretically, there is enough information available to allow anyone to identify (or to confirm the identity of) any organism anywhere in the world, to a suitable level of accuracy. However, in practice it is rare to find the right piece of information in the right format at the right time.
The management of descriptive biological information is made particularly difficult because of:
  1. the wide variety of formats in which such data are recorded
  2. the lack of a standard way of storing and expressing the information in a form that will allow it to be combined, managed, and made available when needed, in the form needed, and
  3. the lack of a collaboration platform that would allow the many custodians of such data around the world to work together.

Why this is so important

Identification of organisms is a critical first step in understanding biodiversity. If the name of an organism is not known, it will be very difficult to find information about it, such as whether the organism is rare or common, poisonous or harmless, of quarantine significance, or ecologically important. Names are the key to storing, finding and retrieving information about organisms. Without the means to name things, the vast amount of available information about any given organism will probably never be discovered.
Equally, the effective management of descriptive information is necessary throughout the science of biology. Unless descriptive information can be managed well and collaboratively, taxonomists, systematists, conservation biologists, quarantine agency staff, researchers, students and the general public will not be able to understand, study, or manage biodiversity effectively.

The Solution

IdentifyLife is a collaborative platform for managing biological descriptive information. It will allow the people who know about organisms to come together to collaboratively record and manage their descriptive information, such as identification keys, natural-language descriptions and coded descriptive data. It will then allow others to search for and find descriptive information when they need to identify an unknown organism, confirm a tentative identification, or simply find out some descriptive facts about an unknown organism.
IdentifyLife Comprises two main parts:
Keys Central is a crowd-sourced index of web-based identification resources such as keys. Owners or users of existing identification keys can contribute a small set of core information – metadata – about their key (such as the key’s title, format, audience, location, geographic scope, the taxa included in it etc). People who need a key – such as a key to the fishes of the Great Barrier Reef, the aphids of Victoria or the ferns of Central Australia – will be able to search Keys Central for just the key they need. Once the index is complete, IdentifyLife’s Keys Central will become an essential place to find keys to any group of organisms anywhere in the world.
The Key to All Life project is an ambitious project to create a collaborative platform capable of handling descriptive information on all the world’s known organisms. With enough information, it would be possible using the Key to All Life to identify any organism from anywhere. IdentifyLife will integrate and combine information from a wide variety of sources into a seamless, giant key and descriptive data resource.
Importantly, IdentifyLife is global in scope and agnostic to the format of data. Both Keys Central and the Key to All Life project will accept any data, in any format, for any organism.
IdentifyLife’s Keys Central is complete and available as part of the Atlas of Living Australia. The Key to All Life project is still under development.

Why IdentifyLife?

IdentifyLife is the only current project that seeks to manage descriptive data collaboratively, and independently from any particular data format. It is being built to be global in scope, but able to be queried for any part of the world. It combines experience from a wide variety of successful previous projects into the most general available system, and uses modern, open-architecture web technologies to allow projects such as the Atlas of Living Australia to freely query its data for any purpose.

The Atlas IdentifyLife Project

The Atlas IdentifyLife project has a number of primary objectives:
Objective 1 Create a crowd-sourced index of existing, web-based identification keys.
Objective 2 Create a repository and mechanism for archiving existing keys, to avoid their descriptive data becoming lost, corrupted or otherwise unavailable to future workers.
Objective 3 Create a collaboration platform for custodians of biological descriptive information to come together to record and manage data about any organism.
Objective 4 Create a key engine at least theoretically capable of being scaleable to flexibly deploy an identification system for all the world’s described living organisms.
Objective 5 Ensure the ongoing viability of the system beyond the funding cycle of the Atlas.

Achievements

The first part of IdentifyLife – the Keys Central global index of identification resources - was released to the public with the Atlas of Living Australia in October 2010. A test node is already operational and production infrastructure is being established. The first step, with Keys Central released, is to engage with the community to crowd-source all available web-based identification resources.
The second major part of IdentifyLife – a platform to facilitate the collaborative management of descriptive data for any organism, is well scoped and under construction.
The core data repositories for the Key to All Life, based on the same data structures used by Google to power its web searches - have been designed, built and partially tested with real data from a set of identification keys. The Key to All Life engine, with which users will be able to follow an identification from the root of life (or from any other starting point) down to (potentially) a species identification, has been scoped and designed. Initial testing of the Key to All Life engine is underway.
