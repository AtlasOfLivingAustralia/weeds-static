I can’t find the species I am looking for

The Atlas has catalogued the names of most Australian species but some searches using valid scientific or common names do not return any matches.

Why is this so?

Scientists have been describing, naming and classifying the world’s plants, animals and microorganisms for over 250 years.  There are currently estimated to be around 1.8 million described species.  All of these have scientific names, and many of them have common names in one or more languages.  Over time, our understanding of the relationships between different populations of organisms has often changed.  As a result, taxonomists create new scientific names to label the species they recognise.  Other taxonomists may come to different conclusions and choose to merge several proposed species into a single species with a single name (and with all the other names now being treated as synonyms).  This means that the number of scientific names which have been created and used is several times greater than the currently accepted number of species.  This problem applies equally to common names. For every scientific name, there could be many common names, and vice-versa. For a fuller explanation of the processes involved in taxonomy and naming organisms, see this video.

Surprisingly, no complete catalogue of all scientific names or common names exists.. A number of major international activities are working to address this need.  In particular, the Catalogue of Life partnership has put together the most complete global list of species (currently including names for 1,293,517 species).

Here in Australia, several important national data sets list the names of Australian species.  In particular, the Australian Plant Name Index (APNI) lists names for Australian plants and provides a consensus view (the Australian Plant Census, APC) of the Australian species (with accepted names and synonyms) for many, but not all, plant families.

Similarly, the Australian Faunal Directory (AFD) provides an essential reference to the names and classification of many animal groups.  Other data sets include names for Australian fungi (ICAF), algae (AMANI, Census of Freshwater Algae) and bryophytes (AUSMOSS).  However, none of these lists provide full coverage for all of the relevant species.

The Atlas uses these Australian resources as its preferred sources for scientific and common names, and draws from the Catalogue of Life when the Australian sources do not cover the group, and for non-Australian species.  Nevertheless, significant gaps exist and many valid scientific names for Australian species will still not be recognised by a search in the Atlas.  Coverage for the best-known common names is likely to be reasonably complete over time, but variations in spelling and punctuation for common names and more localised common names may not be comprehensive.

What are we doing to solve this problem?

The Atlas has provided funding to the Australian Biological Resources Study (ABRS) and the Council of Heads of Australasian Herbaria (CHAH) for taxonomists to add missing groups and names to the existing national species lists (APC, AFD, ICAF, AMANI, Census of Freshwater Algae, AUSMOSS).  Software is being developed to assist taxonomists in this work and to simplify the addition of new names in the future.  By mid-2012, we expect to have the most complete possible catalogue of Australian species and names.

As this work progresses, the Atlas search interfaces will become more complete and accurate.  The Atlas is also working on other interfaces to the same data to simplify the use of authoritative taxonomic information in other web sites and databases.  Australian species names will feed into the Catalogue of Life through the 4D4Life project.

Even with a complete list of species known to have occurred in Australia, the Atlas may not recognise names for newly described species, informal names for undescribed species, names of overseas species and obscure common names.  As the species lists near completion, we will develop interfaces to report additional names.
