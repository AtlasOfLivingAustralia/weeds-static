Some maps show dots outside the real range for the species

The Atlas is producing distribution maps from known records for each species.  In some cases, these maps include surprising errors.

For example

Note: as the Atlas adds more tools to process the data, these examples may already be fixed.

  1. The map for Platypus (Ornithorhynchus anatinus) includes a record far offshore from Queensland.
  2. The map for Great White Shark (Carcharodon carcharias) includes a record well inland in New South Wales.
  3. Quokka (Setonix brachyurus) includes records from outside WA, including one near Sydney.

Why is this so?


The Atlas brings together reported records from many different sources and attempts to present them in ways which support the needs of users.

Some issues arise when the records are first entered into a database. The Platypus record above seems to be an example of this. The locality is given as “LAKE BARRINE, ATHERTON TABLELAND”, which implies that the longitude supplied is around three degrees too far east.  Problems such as this are easily introduced when entering text from a specimen label or enhancing it with information from maps.

Other issues arise because the records may be suitable for some purposes but not for others. The Great White Shark record is an example of this.  The supplied coordinates are 150.0 E, 36.0 S which put it on land. This suggests that the coordinates for this record have been rounded to whole degrees. The record may be useful as part of an analysis examining data at a coarser resolution (e.g. comparing distributions on a one-degree grid). However, it should clearly not be used to determine the preferred habitat for Great White Sharks.

Yet other issues may relate to the context for the record. The Quokka record is an example of this.  The location is well outside the normal range for Quokkas, but the coordinates are in fact correct.  The locality text for this record reads “Ex captive from the Australian Reptile Park”. Similar situations may arise with plants in botanic gardens and insects found in transported produce, to name a few.  Such records may still provide valuable information, particularly when they relate to a specimen in a collection, but are not useful for understanding a species’ distribution or environmental requirements.

The fact that these examples can be seen so easily is a result of the work of the Atlas and its partners in bringing together scattered data sets and enabling them to be  better visualised.  The project will make it easier to find and correct many of these issues.

What we are doing

Over the coming months, we are improving the presentation of these data to allow users to choose either to see all records in their original form, or to filter out records which are unsuitable for modeling species distributions.

  • Analysing harvested data – as the Atlas harvests data from each source, we will run each record through a growing range of automated tests to identify possible issues and annotate the records accordingly. These tests will include:
    • Comparing coordinates with locality names
    • Checking scientific names and higher classifications for consistency
    • Evaluating data sets to determine whether records are aligned to coarse grids
    • Checking for marine species on land and vice versa
    • Identifying records which are significant outliers from the main body of records in terms of their preferred environment (outlier detection).
  • Collecting user annotations – each Atlas page for an occurrence record allows users to annotate the page with proposed comments or corrections.  As we proceed, we expect to offer the opportunity for users to upload batches of comments on multiple records. We are working to feed all of these annotations (and those from automated tests) to data providers in forms which allow them to respond and, where appropriate, incorporate the proposed corrections in their databases.
  • Using confidence thresholds to filter records – automated tools and user annotations will allow us to develop confidence measures for each data set and individual records. We will explore ways to allow users to filter data using these confidence measures.
  • Visualisation of data issues in maps – we will be adding options to the interactive mapping interface to assign different colours to highlight ways that records differ. Among other aspects, this will include:
    • Differentiating records by data provider
    • Separating records with preserved specimens from unvouchered observations
    • Indicating coordinate precision where known
    • Indicating records which are considered to need review for any reason
    • Indicating the locational uncertainty associated with an observervation
  • Including annotations in downloaded data – we will include data quality annotations in spreadsheets of downloaded data. This will allow users to make their own judgments about which records to include in their analyses.
