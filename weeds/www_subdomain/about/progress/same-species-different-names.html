The same species appears under different names

The Atlas is bringing together information from many sources and seeks to link together information provided under different scientific names, where these names are considered to be synonyms of a single species.  However many names which should be treated as synonyms are not covered by our data sources.

For example

Note: as the Atlas adds more tools to process data, these examples may already be fixed.

  1. A search for Dwarf Sperm Whale returns matches for two separate species, under the names Kogia simus (Owen, 1866) and Kogia sima (Owen, 1866) .
  2. A search for Osprey returns matches for White-headed Osprey, Pandion haliaetus (Linnaeus, 1758) and Eastern Osprey, Pandion cristatus (Vieillot, 1816) with similar distribution maps constructed using records from distinct sets of data providers.

Why is this so?

For some general comments on issues around the naming and classification of species, see Species Not Found.

Scientific names and common names all refer to a set of organisms considered to belong to a single species or taxa.  Over time, different names may be applied to the same species.

This may occur with scientific names because:

  1. Two taxonomists may separately describe a species and give it a scientific name.  In time,  taxonomists may recognise these species as the same species and make the later name a synonym of the first.  In this case, the different names may not resemble each other in any way.  As an example, see the many names that have been applied to the Red Kangaroo.
  2. As understanding of the relationships between species develops, taxonomists may need to move a species into a different genus from the one in which it was originally described.  In this case, the names will be in different genera but share the same specific epithet (the second word in a binomial scientific name), or will have a specific epithet which only differs in its ending (e.g. ‘-us’ as opposed to ‘-a’).  As an example, Gould originally named the Shy Albatross Diomedea cauta, but this species has subsequently been moved into a new genus Thalassarche and is consequently now known as Thalassarche cauta (Gould, 1841).
  3. The example of the Dwarf Sperm Whale above with two separate names, one with the ending ‘us’ (Kogia simus) and one with the ending ‘a’ (Kogia sima) is a result of such a move.  The zoological code has required gender agreement (based on the rules for Latin gender) between genus names and specific epithets and in some cases the application of these rules has been unclear, or not properly followed.  As a result, Australian authorities generally use the form Kogia simus and international authorities tend to use Kogia sima.
  4. Taxonomists may also come to the conclusion that a widely-distributed species includes more variation than originally thought and may choose to split it into several different species.  The example of the two Osprey species is a result of such a change.  Until recently Ospreys in Australia were considered to belong to a globally-distributed species (Pandion haliaetus).  In 2008, Christidis and Boles split out the Australian subspecies and now treat it as a full species (Pandion cristatus).  Some Australian data sets have made the corresponding changes to their data sets, while others are still using the name Pandion haliaetus. This is a difficult situation to handle automatically.  Globally there is much information under the name Pandion haliaetus which relates to Ospreys outside Australia, so we cannot simply assume that all information on Pandion haliaetus can be merged with information on Pandion cristatus.
  5. Scientific names are assigned according to strict rules to avoid confusion.  Names must be well-formed, unique, validly published and follow rules based on the Latin language.  For more information, see the International Code of Botanical Nomenclature (ICBN) and the International Code of Zoological Nomenclature (ICZN). Names may be rejected or require correction if they do not fit these rules.
  6. Misspellings of scientific names in publications or the media may result in significant amounts of data and literature being associated with a name that does not match the correct name published in the original description. Sometimes it is hard to establish whether a new spelling is a misspelling or a correction for an error according to the nomenclatural codes.  The Redback Spider was originally named Latrodectus hasseltii in honour of an individual named Hasselt.  The zoological code has rules governing the construction of specific epithets in such cases and hasseltii seems a possibly valid form.  Subsequent authors have often used the form hasselti, either through mistake or an attempted correction.

Application of common names is even more flexible.  The same common name may be applied to several species.  For example, Banjo Shark is applied to Trygonorrhina dumerilii (Castelnau, 1873) and to Trygonorrhina fasciata Müller & Henle, 1841. Similarly many species have many different common names.

The ALA uses reference data sets for authoritative information on the relationships between different names.  In some cases these data sets are missing some important records, which may lead to examples such as the Galah and Redback Spider above.

What are we doing to solve this problem?

The Atlas has provided funding to the Australian Biological Resources Study (ABRS) and the Council of Heads of Australasian Herbaria (CHAH) for taxonomists to add missing groups and names to the existing national species lists (APC, AFD, ICAF, AMANI, Census of Freshwater Algae, AUSMOSS) and to add synonyms where these are missing.  As this work progresses, the Atlas will be able to associate more names with the correct species.

In the meantime, please let us know if you find cases where the right connections haven’t been made between different names.
