23 September, 2011 By Wolf Wanjura

Fish Barcoding

Provided by - Bob Ward, CSIRO Marine and Atmospheric Research, Hobart

We have a medium to long-term aim to barcode all the marine and freshwater fish of Australia. There are probably about 4,500 such species, although the number continues to rise as taxonomic studies progress. There is an Australian regional working group set up through FISH-BOL (www.fishbol.org), with Bob Ward (CSIRO Marine and Atmospheric Research, Hobart) as chair and Martin Gomon (Museum Victoria, Melbourne) as deputy. We would love to welcome further Australian participation in this campaign. Note that there will be a FISH-BOL workshop at the 2011 Adelaide iBOL conference.

To date, most specimens from Australia (and from islands to the north of Australia) have been collected and analysed by CSIRO, often with the assistance of CCDB (Guelph), with significant inputs also from Museum Victoria, the Australian Museum and the Northern Territory Museum. So far, more than 12,000 specimens have been barcoded, from more than 2,500 species, and about 20 publications have been produced (including the first fish barcoding paper, in 2005). Specimens and tissue samples are vouchered at the various museums, although it has not proven possible to voucher each and every specimen barcoded.

Australian fish taxonomists have embraced the barcode approach in their work. Suspected new species with morphological differences have been validated as good species, and likely cryptic species have been detected. It has been used to identify eggs and larvae, and species of sharks in confiscated catches of shark fins.
