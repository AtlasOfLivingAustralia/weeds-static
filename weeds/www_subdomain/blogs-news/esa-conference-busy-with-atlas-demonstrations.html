ESA Conference busy with Atlas demonstrations

6 December, 2011

The ESA Conference was held in Hobart, and the Atlas information booth was busy with visitors interested to know more about the Atlas and whether it could help them in their work. Many ecologists were delighted to find relevant and rich information and tools, all freely available through one site. We heard comments like these regularly, “I wish something like this had been around when I was doing my PhD/last project – it would have saved me months of work”, and “I looked at the Atlas about a year ago, and there wasn’t a lot that was useful to me then. It’s come a long way in the last year; I can use it now.” (these are real comments).

The most common questions asked at the Atlas booth focused on the distribution mapping tools and layers, the number and quality of occurence records, species lists for an area, spatial portal, prediction, scatter plot, data sources and data quality, photos, online data management tools and citizen science applications. A summary of the most commonly asked questions follows:

  • “What information does the Atlas have about species X?”
  • “What information does the Atlas have about the species and/or ecology of area X?”
  • “Does the Atlas include dataset X?”
  • “How can I use the geospatial portal to analyse data?”
  • “How do I use the Atlas to analyse and map my own data?”
  • “Some data points don’t look right; how can I check the validity of the data?”
  • “How do you know the data are reliable?”

We will now be able to write some blog and/or Frequently Asked Questions addressing these.

Many people planned to use their own data with the Atlas mapping tools and layers, once they learnt this was possible. This was a very popular capability. Among the 639 delegates at the conference, we ‘discovered’ a number of potential data sources for insects, marine species, ferals, lizards, flora and zooplankton for the Atlas. We also met a number of delegates who were using the Atlas in their work, and we will follow these people up to find out more and perhaps write more case studies.

Four Atlas representatives were on-hand at the booth to answer questions and provide demonstrations of the Atlas. Many thanks to Margaret Cawsey (Australian National Wildlife Collection), Lee Belbin (Geospatial Team Leader) and Robyn Lawrence (Communications and IT) who spent day and night talking to people and demonstrating the Atlas site. Lee, Margaret and Robyn are all fluent users of the geospatial portal and mapping tools, and were able to show individual ecologists exactly how they could use the Atlas to map and analyse the data they were interested in. These detailed, personalised demonstrations with ecologists were a strong highlight of the conference for the Atlas. Having Lee, Margaret and Robyn on-hand with this sort of geospatial expertise at the ESA Conference was priceless.

Other elements that worked for the Atlas at the ESA Conference:

  1. The Atlas’ data and tools speak for themselves now.
  2. Strong match between the audience and the Atlas’ capabilities.
  3. Kristen Williams’ presentation,”Which environmental layers should I use in my biodiversity model?”  demonstrated Dr Williams’ use of the Atlas in her modelling research and was well received by the audience of about 300. Dr Williams’ presentation is on the Atlas site at http://www.ala.org.au/wp-content/uploads/2010/10/Kristen-Williams-EnvironmentalLayers_ESA2011_21Nov11-LO-RES.pdf.
  4. Dr Ben Raymond’s speed talk, “Exploring patterns and detecting outliers in online biodiversity data” clearly explained how the Atlas can be used to help investigate and analyse data and detect outliers and anomolies in datasets.
  5. Eric Woehler’s poster on “Wind, wind-farms, birds and bats – insights and predictions from the Atlas of Living Australia.” demonstrated ways to use the Atlas data for research.
  6. Two interactive poster sessions were very well attended and located right near the Atlas’ booth. This drew delegates to the Atlas booth, and along with them, more questions, answers and demonstrations.
  7. Having a large screen at the booth worked well for demonstrating the Atlas, especially when small groups of people were watching and learning arond the laptop.
  8. Trays of insects and wildlife and herbarium sheets on display at the booth attracted some delegates. Many thanks to Beth Mantle and Debbie Jennings at ANIC, Margaret Cawsey at ANWC and Bronwyn Collins at ANH for providing beautiful specimens of Australian biodiversity for us to show at the booth.

And again, many thanks to Kristen Williams, Ben Raymond and Eric Woehler for their efforts in using and demonstrating the Atlas.
