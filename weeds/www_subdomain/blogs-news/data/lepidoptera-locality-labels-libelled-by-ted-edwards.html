Lepidoptera Locality Labels Libelled

23 November, 2011

Some labels Ted has discovered in ANIC illustrate his story well

The following article originally appeared in ANIC News, the newsletter of the Australian National Insect Collection, 15: 9-10, in October 1999. The insights presented here remain important to us today as we continue to database Australia’s insect collections.

The Lepidoptera collection in the ANIC contains many labels that require interpretation in order to be fully understood. Valuable information may be lost or labels misunderstood if these interpretations are unrecorded. In particular the accuracy (or inaccuracy) of the labels needs to be understood. By accuracy I do not mean the occasional blunder in spelling or mistaken numeral in the latitude or longitude but the accuracy intended by the collector.

Today’s databases are rapidly making on-line information available to a wide range of uninitiated users who will necessarily take the information at face value. It is essential then that databases faithfully reflect the accuracy of the original label data but this does not always happen. In databasing labels two sources of inaccuracy need to be taken into account. The first concerns the errors inherent in deriving a latitude and longitude for a point a given distance on a bearing from a datum point. The second concerns the accuracy that the collector intended for the label.

Until the late l960’s Lepidoptera labels in ANIC did not give the latitude and longitude. Where a locality is expressed as “37 km E by N of Goulburn” then determining the latitude and longitude based on the compass bearing from Goulburn is prone to error. If the compass points are expressed in the series N, N by E, NNE etc., with an 11.25 degree angle between directions, then at a distance of 18.8 km from the point of reference it is as likely as not that the derived latitude will be in error by one minute (one minute of latitude is approximately 1.85 km). The error will vary depending on the compass bearing. The 18.8 km distance from the reference point is true of an easterly or westerly bearing but will be less in other directions. The derived longitude will be even more prone to error than the latitude, except at the equator, and will increase with increasing latitude and will be at a maximum on a north or south bearing. All this assumes that the point of reference and distance are entirely accurate. While these errors are small we should be aware of them and until I did the calculations I had no idea what their magnitude would be. Clearly a label like “135 km N of Carnarvon WA” cannot be translated into a latitude and longitude that is accurate to the minute without additional information. It is also understood for ANIC labels, but rarely stated, that the distance is a bee-line distance and not a road distance. However specimens from other collections may well be labelled with a road distance.

Dr A.J. Turner and Mr Oswald Lower both found it necessary to give a guide to the accuracy of their labels. Turner explained in an unpublished lecture that his specimens came from an area within a 25 or 30 mile (40-48 km) radius of the town he specified on the label. He considered this accurate enough for coastal and tableland localities but extended it to a 50 or 60 mile (80-96 km) radius for inland areas. Turner also occasionally published a different locality to that given on the label. For example he substituted “Southport” for “Coolangatta”, Qld at a time when the latter was an insignificant hamlet. Lower stated that his “Broken Hill NSW” labels represented anywhere within a five mile (8 km) radius of the town although most were taken at lights within the town.

Many early labels in the Lepidoptera collection have an accuracy similar to those of Lower and it would be misleading to give these a latitude and longitude precise to the minute. Mr Jack Macqueen’s label “Milmerran Q.” (he also spelled it Millmerran, the current map spelling) referred to an area centred on his property at Mt Emlyn about 18 km S of Millmerran. Jack Macqueen’s later “Toowoomba” labels referred to Middle Ridge, Toowoomba. Mr Llew. Gooding labelled specimens “Moe Vic.” although many would have been collected around his dairy farm “Riversleigh Park” near the Tanjil River some miles north of Moe. The “Narara N.S.W.” labels of Mr Leslie Mosse-Robinson referred to an area around his orchard “Exon Vale in the Narara Valley close to Ourimbah”. Mr O. Edwards’ labels “Mitchell, Q.” referred to an area along Womalilla Creek, 25 km S of Mitchell, a region of quite different vegetation to the grasslands surrounding the town. Mr Vic. (Robbie) Robinson’s “Richmond River N.S.W.” labels were based on his collecting in the Rous area. Even at that time “The Big Scrub” had few remaining fragments and two that remain today, Davis Scrub and Victoria Park, were favourite collecting spots of Robbie’s.

The Lepidoptera collection contains a wealth of specimens collected by Robbie. He had two favourite localities that he collected thoroughly and it is worth enlarging on exactly where these were. One, labelled “Mt Keira”, referred to the Mt Keira Scout Camp (34°24′S 150°51′E) at about 320m altitude in the lush subtropical rainforest of the Illawarra escarpment and 1 km W of the Mt Keira summit lookout”. The other he labelled “CSIRO Exp Fm, Wilton, NSW”. In Robbies’ time CSIRO Entomology leased the Cascade Orchard at Wilton from Sydney Water. The Cascade Orchard was established in 1910 by Louis Segal and run by his family for two generations, It is no longer an experiment farm and the orchard no longer exists. The site, 34°16′S 150°44′E some 3 km SE of Wilton and to the west of Cascade Creek, is an interesting junction between Wianamatta Shale and Hawkesbury Sandstone and diverse botanically, a fact of which Robbie was well aware. Another Robbie label has an interesting history. His son Lloyd collected for him in WA and some specimens were labelled “Tarmela”. Lloyd was invited to participate in an expedition organised by the Daily News in 1958 to determine the fate of survivors from the Zuytdorp, a Dutch East lndiaman of the ﬁrst class, wrecked north of Kalbarri in early June 1712. Coins and wreckage had been discovered in 1927 and even in 1958 treasure and many artifacts remained at the site. Lloyd collected moths for his father at the expedition camp near the wreck site, 27°11′S 113°56′E, on Tamala Station.

Sometimes one meets with a “National Park” label. Early this century, if the State was given, these were unambiguous but today the immediate question is which National Park. These labels exist for at least four states. In Qld it means Lamington National Park, in NSW it means Royal National Park, in Tas. it means Mt Field National Park and in WA it means John Forrest National Park.

Some idiosyncratic labels require interpretation. The bold black “M’di” is a rendition of Murrurundi, NSW, by Dr Bertram Middleton, a medical practitioner who lived there. Other common abbreviations are “R’ton” and “T’ba” for Rockharnpton and Toowoomba respectively. Another, found occasionally, is “FeR” for Iron Range, Qld. The Natural History Museum, London has many specimens labelled “Coomoo” which is sometimes published as a locality. It is an abbreviation that Mr George Barnard used last century for his property “Coomooboolaroo” near Duaringa, Qld.

Some labels remind me of the frustration of “Banjo” Patterson when he wrote:

Quite by chance I came across it – ‘Come-by-Chance’ was what I read;
No location was assigned it, nor a thing to help one find it,
Just an N which stood for northward, and the rest was all unsaid.

One day I will collect moths at Come by Chance, NSW. 30°22′S 148°30′E, or better still 18.8 km E by N thereof. It will make an appropriate label.
